# USDA Forest Service Grant Review and Technical Screening

**Project concept reviewed:** Converting forest-thinning / timber-slash wood chips into biochar and useful energy at the InEnTec Technology Center in Richland, Washington.

**Prepared for internal screening**  
**Date:** March 6, 2026

---

## Executive take

The current draft is **not strongest as an electricity-cost-savings story**. In Richland, grid power is relatively inexpensive, while small dual-fuel engine systems running on wood-derived syngas usually have modest electrical efficiency and still require diesel pilot fuel. On a rough screening basis, the **diesel pilot fuel alone can cost as much as or more than the electricity bill being displaced**, before counting biomass handling, drying, labor, maintenance, char handling, engine derating, and downtime.

So the practical move is to **reframe the project around biochar production, wildfire-risk reduction, and innovative use of forest restoration residues**, with the syngas used **primarily as internal process heat** rather than as the centerpiece power product. That framing is materially stronger for grant fit and materially more honest technically.

---

## 1) Grant fit: where this concept can qualify

### A. Wood Innovations Grant — plausible fit, but only with the right framing

The FY 2026 Wood Innovations funding opportunity says it is intended to expand wood energy and wood products markets, reduce wildfire risk, reduce forest-management costs, and promote community economic and environmental health. It specifically mentions projects such as developing manufacturing capacity, wood-products infrastructure, wood-energy clusters, and overcoming market barriers in the commercial sector. Applications are due **April 22, 2026** and cost sharing is required. citeturn4view0

**How this project could qualify under Wood Innovations:**

- Use **forest thinning and restoration residues** as feedstock, directly tying the project to wildfire-risk reduction and forest health. citeturn4view0
- Position the project as an **innovative wood product / market expansion** effort centered on **biochar** as the saleable product.
- Emphasize **commercialization barriers being solved**: feed handling, moisture tolerance, char quality control, emissions control, product qualification, and supply-chain development.
- Frame the Richland site as a **demonstration and market-enabling facility** for sawmills, farmers, ports, and restoration contractors.

**What weakens the fit:**

- Making the narrative mainly about cheaper electricity. The economics are not compelling at your scale.
- Presenting it as early-stage concepting without a specific deployment pathway. The notice says early-phase project development will not be competitive. citeturn4view0

### B. Community Wood Grant — possible only if it is truly shovel-ready

The FY 2026 Community Wood Energy and Wood Innovation Program is for **shovel-ready** projects that install a community wood energy system or build an innovative wood product facility. The maximum award is **$1.0 million** covering up to **35% of capital costs**, with up to **$1.5 million** and up to **50% of capital costs** in special cases. citeturn1view1

**How this project could qualify under Community Wood:**

- As an **innovative wood product facility** producing biochar from secondary woody material.
- As a capital project that installs a **commercial biochar production line** with defined equipment, feed handling, emissions controls, commissioning plan, and operating plan.
- Only if it is actually **shovel-ready**: site identified, equipment list defined, vendor quotes in hand, process flow diagram completed, permitting path understood, match secured, schedule credible.

**What weakens the fit:**

- If the design is still exploratory.
- If the proposal depends on proving out engine economics instead of deploying a ready-to-build product facility.
- If the requested federal share exceeds the program’s percentage limit. citeturn1view1

### C. Likely better strategic fit than either of the above: WPIA

There is also a **2026 Wood Products Infrastructure Assistance (WPIA)** program aimed at facilities that purchase and process byproducts from ecosystem restoration / forest-management projects on federal or Tribal lands, specifically to increase market opportunities and value for those byproducts. citeturn3search6

That may actually fit a biochar-production facility better than an “electricity from syngas” pitch, especially if the feedstock can be tied to restoration residues from federal or Tribal projects.

## 2) Recommended reframing of the project

### Recommended primary story

**“Forest-restoration residues to qualified biochar, with internal thermal energy recovery.”**

That means:

- **Primary product:** biochar
- **Primary grant outcomes:** wildfire-risk reduction, utilization of low-value residues, local manufacturing, carbon storage in durable biochar, agricultural soil benefits, demonstration of a deployable residue-to-product pathway
- **Energy story:** use process gases internally for thermal support and parasitic-load reduction, **not** as the central economic justification

### Recommended secondary story

**Demonstration platform for modular deployment at sawmills / agricultural sites**

That is much more believable than claiming a small system will beat grid electricity pricing in Washington.

### Story to avoid

**“We will make cheap electricity from woody syngas and materially reduce power costs.”**

That story is exactly where the math starts throwing rocks.

## 3) Rough screening mass and energy balance

The numbers below are **screening-level assumptions**, not final design numbers.

### Base electrical load assumption

- Average facility electric demand to be offset: **25 kW**
- Daily electricity demand: **25 kW × 24 h = 600 kWh/day**
- Monthly electricity demand: **18,000 kWh/month**

### Generator case assumptions

Assume a small dual-fuel engine-generator using cleaned wood syngas plus diesel pilot fuel:

- Net electrical efficiency: **25%** (screening assumption; real systems can be worse)
- Diesel share of fuel energy: **20%**
- Syngas share of fuel energy: **80%**

Then fuel energy required is:

- **600 kWh/day ÷ 0.25 = 2,400 kWh/day fuel input**
- Equivalent thermal input: **8.64 GJ/day**

Split by fuel:

- Diesel input: **1.73 GJ/day**
- Wood-derived syngas input: **6.91 GJ/day**

### Diesel requirement

Using diesel lower heating value of about **138.7 MJ/gal**, pilot diesel use is:

- **1.73 GJ/day ÷ 138.7 MJ/gal ≈ 12.5 gal/day**
- About **375 gal/month**

### Biomass requirement

For the syngas portion, the wood requirement depends strongly on moisture content and conversion performance. A rough dry-wood equivalent based on **18 MJ/kg dry wood** is:

- **6.91 GJ/day ÷ 18 MJ/kg ≈ 384 kg/day dry wood**

Equivalent as-fed wood requirement:

- At ~20% moisture equivalent energy content: about **480 kg/day**
- At poorer fuel quality: can easily rise toward **575 kg/day** or more

So a fair screening range is:

- **0.38 to 0.58 metric tons/day of wood feed**
- Roughly **11 to 17 metric tons/month**

### Biochar output estimate

This is where the process configuration matters.

#### Case A — air gasification optimized for gas + engine power

Typical char yield is often only on the order of **5–10 wt% of dry feed**.

Using 384 kg/day dry feed:

- Biochar: roughly **19 to 38 kg/day**
- Monthly biochar: roughly **0.6 to 1.2 metric tons/month**

That is not much char. In other words: once you optimize for engine fuel, the biochar story shrinks.

#### Case B — biochar-first thermal conversion / retort style operation

If the system is designed primarily for biochar production rather than maximum gas yield, char yield may be more like **20–30 wt% of dry feed**.

Using 384 kg/day dry feed:

- Biochar: roughly **77 to 115 kg/day**
- Monthly biochar: roughly **2.3 to 3.5 metric tons/month**

That is a much more plausible basis for a biochar-centered proposal.

## 4) Rough operating cost comparison: grid vs dual-fuel power

### Grid electricity cost in Richland / Benton PUD area

Benton PUD’s posted small general service rate shows an energy charge of **$0.0622/kWh**, a demand charge of **$1.05/kW**, and a daily system charge of **$0.86/day** for multi-phase service. Washington’s statewide average retail electricity price is around **10.13 cents/kWh**, and some local bill-comparison sources place Richland around **11 cents/kWh**. citeturn1view2turn6search0turn0search5

So for **18,000 kWh/month**:

- Using Benton small general service energy + demand + daily charge:
  - Energy: **18,000 × $0.0622 = $1,119.60/month**
  - Demand: **25 × $1.05 = $26.25/month**
  - Daily charge: about **$25.80/month**
  - **Total ≈ $1,172/month** before taxes and miscellaneous charges

A more conservative “blended bill reality” range using 10–11 cents/kWh is about:

- **$1,820 to $1,980/month**

### Diesel pilot fuel cost alone

Washington diesel prices have been high. EIA’s Washington diesel page was updated **March 3, 2026**, and Reuters reported the U.S. average diesel price reached **$4.04/gal** on March 4, 2026; a Washington-specific February report cited about **$4.79/gal** in Washington. citeturn1view3turn0news42turn0search6

At about **375 gal/month** of pilot diesel:

- At **$4.04/gal**: about **$1,510/month**
- At **$4.79/gal**: about **$1,790/month**

That is the killer detail: **diesel pilot fuel alone is in the same range as, or higher than, the likely electricity bill being displaced.**

And that is **before** adding:

- wood procurement / grinding / screening / drying
- operator time
- maintenance
- lube oil and filters
- char handling
- tar / particulate cleanup upkeep
- generator derating and downtime
- capital recovery

### Bottom line on economics

On a pure electricity replacement basis, **this is very unlikely to beat the grid in Richland**.

That does **not** kill the project.

It means the economic center of gravity must be:

1. **biochar product value**,
2. **forest residue utilization / wildfire reduction**,
3. **grant-funded demonstration value**, and
4. **possible thermal self-sufficiency / resilience**,

—not simple electric bill savings.

## 5) Carbon view

### Grid electricity baseline

Washington’s 2024 electricity profile lists about **249 lb CO2/MWh**. For **18 MWh/month**, that is roughly:

- **4,482 lb CO2/month**
- about **2.03 metric tons CO2/month**

Washington grid electricity is relatively low-carbon already. citeturn6search0

### Dual-fuel generator case

EIA lists diesel at about **22.45 lb CO2/gal**. At **375 gal/month**, diesel pilot combustion alone gives about:

- **8,420 lb CO2/month**
- about **3.8 to 3.9 metric tons CO2/month**

That already exceeds the grid-electricity baseline for the same electrical output. citeturn7search0

Biomass carbon is often counted separately as biogenic, but from a screening standpoint the project should **not** be sold as an obvious near-term CO2 win on electricity generation alone.

### Biochar carbon storage

USDA Climate Hubs describes biochar as a **stable solid rich in carbon**, and USDA / NRCS guidance notes low-H:C biochar is preferred to maximize soil carbon sequestration; USDA Northeast Climate Hubs notes low-H:C biochar can remain in soil for **hundreds of years and more**. citeturn8search3turn8search0turn8search10

That is the carbon case that actually works here.

A rough screening approach is:

- Assume biochar is **~80% carbon** by mass
- Assume **~80% of that carbon is stable long-term**
- CO2e stored ≈ **biochar mass × 2.35**

Then approximate monthly storage is:

- **Power-first gasification case (0.6–1.2 t biochar/month):** about **1.4–2.8 tCO2e/month** stored
- **Biochar-first case (2.3–3.5 t biochar/month):** about **5.4–8.2 tCO2e/month** stored

So the carbon story becomes much stronger when the process is optimized for **biochar yield and quality**, not for engine fuel.

## 6) Technical review of the proposed alternative: make biochar first, then burn the syngas around the unit for process heat

## Short answer

**Yes — this is the more technically suitable approach if the real goal is biochar.**

It is also much easier to defend in a grant application.

### Why it is more suitable

1. **Direct heat use is easier than engine use.**  
   Low-Btu gas from air-blown gasification is much more forgiving when burned in a thermal oxidizer / furnace / annular combustion chamber than when cleaned and fed to an engine.

2. **You avoid the weakest link in the economics.**  
   Engine efficiency, derating, fuel-quality sensitivity, and diesel pilot consumption are where the money goes to die.

3. **It aligns with biochar-first design logic.**  
   If the process is intended to maximize char retention, then using released volatiles / syngas as internal process heat is standard and sensible.

4. **It reduces gas-cleaning complexity.**  
   Gas for direct combustion generally needs much less polishing than gas for engine service.

5. **It supports the grant story better.**  
   You can truthfully say the project converts low-value residues into a durable soil-carbon product while using internally generated gases to reduce auxiliary fuel demand.

### Important caveats

This approach is suitable **only if the reactor is designed and controlled correctly**. Main constraints:

- **Oxygen leakage / over-oxidation risk:** too much air and you burn up char instead of making it.
- **Temperature control:** too hot lowers char yield; too cold creates unstable product and more condensables.
- **Feed moisture:** wet slash is not impossible, but “no extensive predrying needed” is the kind of sentence that later tries to murder your budget. High moisture hurts stability and char quality.
- **Product consistency:** agriculture / carbon-market pathways need repeatable char specs.
- **Air emissions / permitting:** combustion chamber design, particulate control, opacity, and startup/shutdown emissions matter.
- **Ash and alkali management:** slagging / fouling / fines carryover can become annoying very quickly.
- **Continuous solids handling:** char discharge, cooling, fire prevention, and dust control need to be designed in, not wished in.

### Practical conclusion on this configuration

If the objective is **biochar plus internal thermal efficiency**, then a **biochar-first retort / carbonizer with direct combustion of process gas for reactor heat** is a much more credible pathway than “gasifier to engine-generator to save electricity.”

If the objective is **lowest-cost electricity**, this is still not the right hill to die on in Richland.

## 7) Key constraints that should be stated plainly

### Commercial / economic constraints

- Grid electricity in Washington is already cheap relative to small biomass power.
- Dual-fuel engine operation still consumes material diesel.
- Small systems are punished by labor, uptime, fuel prep, and maintenance.
- Carbon-credit upside is optional, uncertain, and should not be the primary financial crutch.

### Technical constraints

- Feedstock variability: size, ash, bark fraction, dirt contamination, and moisture.
- Need for reliable char-quality testing and specification.
- Air permitting and emissions-control requirements.
- Need for realistic O&M assumptions, not heroic assumptions.

### Grant constraints

- Wood Innovations: stronger if focused on **market expansion / commercialization barrier removal / demonstration**. citeturn4view0
- Community Wood: stronger only if **shovel-ready** and capex-defined. citeturn1view1turn2view4
- WPIA: strong if feedstock is linked to **restoration byproducts from federal or Tribal lands**. citeturn3search6

## 8) Recommended proposal direction

### Best-positioned concept

**Proposed title direction:**  
**Forest Residues to Qualified Biochar with Internal Thermal Energy Recovery**

### Core claims to make

- Utilizes low-value woody residues from thinning / restoration / slash reduction
- Creates a saleable innovative wood product: biochar
- Supports forest health and wildfire-risk reduction
- Demonstrates a modular pathway relevant to sawmills, ports, farms, and restoration contractors
- Uses internally generated gases to reduce auxiliary thermal demand
- Provides measurable carbon-storage benefits through durable biochar

### Claims to soften or remove

- “Affordable electricity” as the lead economic argument
- Strong claims that wet slash can be processed without meaningful drying penalty
- Any implication that the engine-generator route is the lowest-cost energy solution at this site

---

## 9) One-paragraph conclusion for internal use

From a technical and economic screening perspective, the project is **credible as a biochar and forest-residue utilization project**, but **weak as a power-cost-savings project**. For a 25 kW average load in Richland, WA, the diesel pilot fuel required for dual-fuel syngas generation alone can approach or exceed the monthly cost of utility electricity, before including biomass handling and maintenance. The concept becomes much stronger if it is reframed around **biochar production, wildfire-risk reduction, market development for wood residues, and internal use of process gas for heat**, rather than around low-cost power generation. On that basis, the project appears potentially competitive for **Wood Innovations** and possibly **Community Wood** if shovel-ready, with **WPIA** also worth reviewing if federal/Tribal restoration feedstocks are central.
